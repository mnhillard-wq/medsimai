Module Overview

What is ambiguity in medicine?

Uncertainty is medicine's baseline — but training has long rewarded certainty, leaving many of us feeling that "I don't know" is a failure rather than an honest clinical response. These cases are a chance to practice something different: sitting with what isn't yet known, communicating it clearly to a family, and offering a thoughtful plan even without an answer.

The module includes brief introductory material followed by three practice cases. In each one, you'll interact with an AI-standardized parent of a pediatric patient facing a different type of uncertainty — and with it, different needs.

Types of clinical ambiguity

Ambiguity in clinical practice takes different forms. The three cases in this module each represent a distinct type.

Unexplained ambiguity The workup is complete and unremarkable — no cause is found. The challenge is communicating that this may be the only answer. (Case 1)
Protocol-driven ambiguity Serious action before a diagnosis is confirmed. The family sees intervention without explanation. (Case 2)
Diagnostic ambiguity under pressure The differential includes serious diagnoses, no answer yet, and the emotional stakes are high. (Case 3)
Prognostic uncertainty The diagnosis is known, but the outcome is not. Honest prognosis — without false hope or false doom — is its own skill.
Therapeutic uncertainty The evidence is limited or conflicting and the right treatment path is genuinely unclear. Shared decision-making becomes essential.

Principles to carry across all cases

Acting with confidence while simultaneously holding uncertainty is what expert clinical practice actually looks like. The goal isn't to feel certain before you walk in — it's to communicate honestly and offer a clear path forward in its absence.

Principle What this looks like
Name uncertainty directly Use clear language to state what is not known; do not imply a diagnosis or certainty that does not exist
Acknowledge the family's emotional response Validate the parent's distress; create space for emotion before moving to clinical information
Hold genuine uncertainty without minimizing it Do not imply more certainty than the evidence supports; resist the urge to reassure prematurely
Offer a clear plan even without a diagnosis Describe next steps — monitoring, follow-up, what to watch for — so the family leaves with direction
Communicate that you will not abandon them Make clear the team will remain present and supportive regardless of whether an answer is found

Frameworks

Frameworks for communicating uncertainty

Now that you've done Case 1, here are two frameworks to carry into the next cases. RAPS is about orienting yourself — noticing where you and the family each stand relative to the uncertainty before you walk in. ADAPT is about the conversation itself: what to say, and in what order.

RAPS — Orienting yourself to uncertainty

RAPS addresses how you orient to uncertainty — not just in the room, but before and after. It was developed by clinician-educators at Harvard Medical School and published in the Journal of General Internal Medicine (2024). Before each case, use the grid below to locate yourself: where are you sitting relative to the family, and how should that shape how you walk in?

RRecognize — Notice that uncertainty is present — for you, for the patient, or both. The parent may be far more certain than you are, or far more uncertain. Awareness of this asymmetry changes how you enter the conversation.
AAcknowledge — Name, accept, and normalize the uncertainty — both internally and with the patient. Acknowledging uncertainty is an act of clinical honesty that deepens trust, not a sign of weakness.
PPartner — Invest in the therapeutic alliance and co-construct a plan. Emphasize non-abandonment: "I don't know exactly what is going on, but I will be with you." Certainty in the relationship replaces certainty in the diagnosis.
SSeek Support — After the encounter, reflect and debrief. Uncertainty is not a private burden — sharing it normalizes the experience and drives learning.

ADAPT — A framework for disclosing uncertainty

ADAPT gives structure to the actual conversation — what to say, and in what order, when you're disclosing uncertainty to a patient or family. It was tested with medical students and surgical residents and found to meaningfully improve confidence in these conversations. The mnemonic walks you through five steps.

A
Assess the patient's current understanding Before you explain anything, find out what the family already knows or believes. This shapes how you frame information and prevents you from talking past their actual concerns. "Can you tell me what you understand about what happened and what the team has told you so far?"
D
Disclose your uncertainty. Be direct. Name the uncertainty explicitly and early. Vagueness is not kindness — it creates anxiety. Use clear, plain language. "I want to be honest with you: we don't have a definitive answer for what caused this."
A
Acknowledge and respond to patient emotions After disclosing uncertainty, pause. The family will have an emotional reaction — fear, frustration, disbelief. Make space for it before moving forward. "I imagine that's really hard to hear. What are you feeling right now?"
P
Plan next steps with the patient Even without a diagnosis, there is always a plan. Walk the family through what happens next — monitoring, follow-up, what to watch for, when to return. Making the plan collaborative reinforces partnership. "Here is what we can do from here, and I'd like to know if this makes sense to you."
T
Temper expectations Be honest about what the future may or may not bring. Avoid implying that the next test or the next visit will provide the answer if it may not. "I want you to know that even with follow-up, we may not find a cause — and that is actually a common outcome with this type of event."
ADAPT in practice — Case 1 example language click to expand
A — Assess "Lauren, before I go through the results, I want to make sure I understand what the team has explained to you. Can you tell me what you know so far about what happened with Mia?"
D — Disclose "I want to be upfront with you: we have done a complete workup and we haven't found a cause. This type of event — we call it a BRUE — often doesn't have an identifiable explanation."
A — Acknowledge "I can imagine how frightening that is to hear, especially after what you watched happen. This wasn't your fault, and your instinct to bring her in was exactly right."
P — Plan "Here is what we want to do: we're going to monitor Mia overnight, and before you go home, we'll go through what to watch for and when to come back. We'll also arrange a close follow-up visit."
T — Temper "I do want to be honest — even at follow-up, we may not find a definitive explanation. That's actually what happens in the majority of these cases. Our plan is designed to keep Mia safe whether or not we ever have an answer."

One thing worth naming before you go in: most students worry that admitting uncertainty will shake the family's confidence or make them seem less competent. The evidence suggests the opposite — when uncertainty is disclosed clearly and paired with a specific plan, it tends to strengthen the clinical relationship rather than undermine it. The admission itself isn't the risk; it's admitting it without a path forward that erodes trust.

Claudia — An 18-day-old with fever

Patient: Claudia, 18 days old  |  Parent: Arthur Lopez (first-time father)  |  Setting: Inpatient pediatrics — admitted during sepsis workup

Claudia has a fever at 18 days old and is undergoing a full sepsis workup — LP, cultures, empiric IV antibiotics — despite looking relatively well. Jack — An 8-year-old with abnormal labs

Patient: Jack Wyles, age 8  |  Parent: Irene Wyles (Jack's mother)  |  Setting: Inpatient pediatrics — admitted for monitoring

Jack is admitted with persistent fever and abnormal labs; the differential includes leukemia but no diagnosis has been made. Review sample language

Once you've done all three encounters, the sample ADAPT language for Cases 2 and 3 is below — for reflection after the fact, not as a script going in.

ADAPT in practice — Case 2 example language (Arthur Lopez / Claudia) click to expand

A — Assess: "Arthur, before I explain what we're doing and why, I want to make sure I understand what the team has already shared with you. Can you tell me what you know so far about why Claudia was admitted?"

D — Disclose: "I want to be honest with you: we don't have an infection confirmed yet — and we may not find one. But with a baby Claudia's age who has a fever, we have to act as if there could be a serious infection until we can prove otherwise."

A — Acknowledge: "I can see this is really frightening — watching your daughter go through all of this when she seems okay is genuinely hard. Your concern for her is clear, and asking these questions is exactly right."

P — Plan: "Here is what is happening: we are watching her closely, the cultures are running, and we expect results over the next 24 to 48 hours. We will update you as soon as we know more."

T — Temper: "It's possible that all of the cultures come back negative — and if they do, that is good news, but it also means we may never know exactly what caused the fever. We are prepared for that, and we will talk through what it means for Claudia's care as results come in."

ADAPT in practice — Case 3 example language (Irene Wyles / Jack) click to expand

A — Assess: "Irene, I know you've been through so much already with Emma. Before I go through Jack's results, I want to understand — what are you most worried about right now, and what has the team explained to you so far?"

D — Disclose: "I want to be direct with you about where we are: Jack's labs are abnormal, and we are taking that seriously. We are still working to understand what is causing them. I cannot tell you yet what the diagnosis is, and I don't want to speculate before we have more information."

A — Acknowledge: "I know that uncertainty may be the hardest thing to hear, especially given what your family has already been through with Emma. It makes complete sense that your mind would go to the places it's going right now."

P — Plan: "Here is what we are doing: we have additional tests running and we have asked the right specialists to be involved. We will have more information within the next day or two, and we will sit down with you as soon as we do."

T — Temper: "I want you to know that I cannot rule anything in or out right now — and I won't pretend otherwise. What I can promise is that we will not leave you in the dark. We will share information with you as we have it, and we will get through this step by step together."
