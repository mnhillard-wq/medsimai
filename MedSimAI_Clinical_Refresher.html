Section 1

What is MedSimAI?

MedSimAI is an AI-powered clinical simulation platform developed at Cornell University that hosts cases across clinical settings, specialty areas, and levels of training. For this course, faculty and students have selected and developed 10 cases specifically designed to target the skills you will need entering clerkships.

About this guide This guide is intended to orient you to the series and provide quick access to review resources — interview frameworks, presentation structure, note-writing tips, and the full case list. We suggest completing these cases before attending your CSC clerkship refresher and before starting your first clerkship rotations.

Skills to feel comfortable with before clerkship (goal: familiarity, not mastery)

  • Taking and presenting a focused or interval history
  • Asking targeted follow-up questions based on a working differential
  • Generating a reasonable differential and supporting your reasoning
  • Committing to a leading diagnosis while staying open to revision
  • Putting together a concise assessment and a basic problem-based plan
  • Engaging in clinical discussion, including asking questions when unsure

How Cases Work

  1. Review case instructions
    Overview of what you will do in the encounter.
  2. Review the door note
    Patient name, setting, reason for visit, and the specific task you are being asked to perform.
  3. Review the chart
    Available before you enter the encounter and at any point during it to review labs, vitals, and prior information.
  4. Engage in the encounter
    Voice or text. Multi-step cases may include patients, family members, and physicians, each with their own instructions.
  5. Complete follow-up activities
    Note writing, if required for the case.
  6. Review your feedback
    Provided for history-taking, communication, and/or documentation skills, along with take-home learning resources.
  7. Share with a mentor (optional)
    Use the share encounter button to send your performance to a faculty mentor. Note a specific question if you have one, or flag technical issues or case content concerns.

Cases can be repeated at any time to practice a different approach or revisit after receiving feedback.

Feedback is appreciated We are actively developing this series to fit the needs of students. Your input will directly shape what comes next — cases you found unhelpful, features you would like to see, things that worked well, or technical issues you encountered.

Section 3

The Complete History

A complete history has a consistent structure. The following components apply across specialties, with some variation in emphasis depending on the clinical context.

A note on clerkships In most clinical settings, you will rarely — if ever — be asked to gather all of these components in a single encounter. The value of reviewing the complete history is familiarity: knowing what questions exist so you can recognize which ones matter most for the patient in front of you, whether that is a focused urgent visit, a new patient intake, or an annual physical.
History of Present Illness (HPI)
Narrative account of the chief concern — onset, duration, severity, location, quality, modifying factors, associated symptoms, and how it has evolved over time.
Past Medical and Surgical History
Relevant diagnoses, hospitalizations, and surgeries. For each: when established, how managed, and any complications.
Medications and Allergies
All medications (Rx, OTC, supplements) with doses — confirm what the patient actually takes. For each allergy, document the specific reaction.
Family History
First-degree relatives with relevant diagnoses — note the condition, relative, and age of onset.
Social and Exposure History
Occupation, housing, social supports, tobacco/alcohol/drug use, sexual history, travel, and exposures — depth calibrated to the differential.
Review of Systems (ROS)
Structured screen across organ systems for symptoms not raised in the HPI — not a repeat of the HPI, but a targeted check for related or comorbid problems.

The Focused History

In focused encounters, the same structural components apply but depth is calibrated to the clinical question. The categories below provide a framework for hypothesis-driven inquiry — the presenting concern and your emerging differential determine which to prioritize.

Symptom course Pace of illness (worsening, improving, stable) · Therapeutics tried · Aggravating and alleviating factors · Prior similar episodes

Example Focused History Checklist — Febrile Infant click to expand
#CategoryWhat to ask / why it matters
1 Fever characterization "How did you measure the temperature?" / "Was it taken rectally?" / "What was the reading?"
Rectal temperature is the standard in infants. ≥38.0 °C rectal meets the threshold for full evaluation in young infants.
2 Feeding history "How has feeding been today?" / "Same amount as usual?" / "Is she waking up to feed?"
Feeding is one of the earliest behaviors affected by illness. Poor feeding can be an early sign of systemic infection.
3 Red flag symptoms "Has she been more lethargic or unusually irritable?" / "Any trouble breathing?" / "Any rash or blisters?" / "Any seizure-like movements?"
Lethargy or marked irritability → meningitis/sepsis. Respiratory distress → pneumonia/sepsis. Vesicular rash → neonatal HSV. Seizure-like activity → CNS infection (meningitis, HSV encephalitis).
4 Associated symptoms "Any vomiting or diarrhea?" / "Congestion or cough?" / "Any change in wet diapers or urine output?"
Vomiting/diarrhea → GI infection. Congestion/cough → viral respiratory infection. Decreased urine output → dehydration.
5 Birth and perinatal history "Was she full term?" / "Any complications at delivery or afterward?" / "Was she in the NICU?"
Prematurity increases susceptibility to infection. A complicated nursery stay may indicate prior perinatal complications.
6 Maternal GBS status "Do you know if mom was tested for Group B Strep during pregnancy?" / "Did she need antibiotics during labor?"
Untreated maternal GBS colonization is associated with higher rates of neonatal early-onset sepsis.
7 HSV / herpes exposure "Does mom or anyone at home have herpes or cold sores?" / "Were there any active sores at the time of delivery?" / "Any blisters on the baby?"
HSV exposure during delivery or from caregivers (HSV-1) can cause serious neonatal infection that may initially present as fever only.
8 Sick contacts and exposures "Is anyone sick at home?" / "Any recent contact with someone who was ill?"
Sick household contacts increase the likelihood of viral infections in the infant.
Tips for focused histories
  • Consider a differential diagnosis before entering the room (include common and "can't miss" conditions); refine or expand this as you gather more history
  • Characterize the chief concern (onset, progression, key features)
  • Ask about associated symptoms, including those that could suggest a "can't miss" diagnosis — even if unlikely
  • Identify red flags relevant to the presentation
  • Review the medication list with the patient (confirm what they are actually taking and identify clinical relevance)
  • Target family and social history to relevant risk factors (do not need to be exhaustive)
  • Elicit the patient's perspective ("What do you think is going on?" / "What worries you most?")

Specialty-specific considerations (focused history)

PsychiatryAssess mood, affect, thought process/content, and safety (SI/HI). Screen for substance use and prior psychiatric history. Establish confidentiality boundaries early.
PediatricsIntegrate caregiver and child perspectives. Review birth history, development, immunizations, and feeding. Gauge caregiver concerns and reliability of history.
Obstetrics / GynecologyClarify pregnancy status (LMP, contraception, sexual activity). Obtain obstetric and gynecologic history. Screen for ectopic risk and safety concerns.
NeurologyLocalize symptoms precisely — onset, progression, and focal deficits. Assess associated features (weakness, numbness, speech, vision, gait) and establish baseline function.
SurgeryFocus on acuity and trajectory. Identify red flags (peritonitis, obstruction, bleeding). Confirm NPO status, last oral intake, and anticoagulation use.
Emergency MedicinePrioritize onset and acuity — ask about red flags first. Establish key risk factors quickly (cardiac history, prior clots, immunocompromise) and confirm medications and allergies early.

The Interval Inpatient History

Rather than building a history from scratch, the interval history focuses on what has changed and what remains unresolved — built from chart review before you enter the room and a focused bedside conversation with the patient.

Section 4

Oral Presentations

An oral presentation is a structured synthesis delivered to a listener. It is curated and interpretive — not a verbatim account of the encounter. Its purpose is to transfer clinical understanding efficiently.

The one-liner

A single sentence that orients the listener — who the patient is, what brought them in, and one or two contextual factors that shape the differential. It is a frame, not a diagnosis.

Example: "Jordan is a 72-year-old man on adalimumab for rheumatoid arthritis presenting with one day of worsening left lower quadrant pain."

History of Present Illness

Brief and narrative. Include the high-yield positives and the clinically meaningful negatives. Not everything you asked — the information that matters for the differential you are building.

Past history, medications, family, social

Pertinent items only. The attending knows the structure of a complete history; they want to know which elements of the past history are relevant to this presentation and why.

Physical exam

Vitals first, every time. Then the relevant exam findings. When you report a finding, attach its significance: "voluntary guarding but no rebound tenderness" carries different diagnostic weight than "abdominal tenderness."

Assessment & Problem Representation

Your clinical interpretation, structured in two parts: a problem representation followed by a prioritized differential. For a step-by-step breakdown and worked example, see the Assessment & Problem Representation section below — the same framework applies to your oral presentation and your note.

Plan

Organized by problem. Each item should trace directly back to the assessment. For a worked example and guidance on structure, see the Plan section below.

Specialty-specific Communication

Certain specialties require adjustments to both how you take a history and how you structure your presentation and note. The considerations below apply across all activities in specialty cases.

Obstetrics / GynecologyInclude gestational age and obstetric context in the one-liner. Differential, urgency, and plan shift by trimester. Safety considerations belong in the assessment, not as an afterthought.
PsychiatryMSE findings are core objective data. The assessment integrates diagnostic criteria, safety, and insight. Avoid leading with a label before establishing the supporting features.
PediatricsName your source of history (parent, child, or both). Include age in months or years in the one-liner. Developmental context and caregiver concerns are part of the clinical picture.
Inpatient MedicineInclude hospital day and presenting diagnosis in the one-liner. Follow interval format — what changed, what the data show, what the plan is for today.
Emergency MedicineLead with the presenting concern and time-sensitive elements. Include disposition thinking in the assessment. Name your rule-out explicitly when evidence is still pending.

Presenting to your AI-Attending

What to expect When the attending says "go ahead," deliver your full presentation — one-liner through plan — without waiting for prompts. A complete presentation, even an imperfect one, gives more to work with and more to learn from. The attending will push back, probe your reasoning, and ask you to support your thinking. This is deliberate: the goal is to help you arrive at the answer through questions, not to give it to you. For medical content they are a resource — ask if you are stuck. For presentation structure, work toward it yourself.
Deep Dive

Assessment & Problem Representation

The assessment is the interpretive core of both your oral presentation and your clinical note. It is where you move from data to reasoning — synthesizing what you gathered into a clinical picture that drives the differential and plan. It is structured in two parts: a problem representation followed by a prioritized differential.

Problem representation is a one- to two-sentence synthesis of the case using medical terminology. It translates raw data — symptoms, vitals, demographic context, key risk factors — into a clinical pattern that activates the appropriate differential. It does not name a diagnosis; it sets up the diagnostic reasoning that follows.

Step 1 — Problem Representation
"72-year-old immunocompromised man (on a biologic for rheumatoid arthritis) presenting with one day of acute-onset, constant focal LLQ pain, low-grade fever, and tachycardia — a pattern consistent with an acute colonic process."

Differential: Name your leading diagnosis and support it with at least two findings from the history or exam. Then give two or three alternatives — for each, note why it belongs on the list and why it is less likely than your leading diagnosis.

Step 2 — Differential
Leading: Acute diverticulitis — classic LLQ localization with fever and tachycardia; immunosuppression may blunt exam findings.

Alternatives:
  • Colonic malignancy with perforation — on differential given age and family history of colon cancer; less likely because this is an acute focal presentation.
  • Infectious colitis — on differential given biologic use; less likely because no diarrhea.
  • Ischemic colitis — on differential given age and hemodynamic instability; less likely because no hematochezia.
Together — Assessment
72-year-old immunocompromised man on adalimumab presenting with one day of acute LLQ pain and fever. Vitals are notable for tachycardia (HR 102) and mild hypotension, raising concern for early septic shock. Most consistent with acute diverticulitis — LLQ localization, fever, and hemodynamic changes are classic; immunosuppression may blunt exam and laboratory findings.

Differential also includes colonic malignancy with perforation (on list given age and family history of colon cancer at 65; less likely because this is an acute focal process without mass or obstruction symptoms), infectious colitis (on list given biologic use; less likely because no diarrhea), and ischemic colitis (on list given age and hemodynamic instability; less likely because no hematochezia).

In an oral presentation

The assessment follows the physical exam and precedes the plan. Deliver the problem representation aloud as a single statement, then walk through your differential — leading diagnosis first, supported by specific findings, then alternatives with your reasoning for each. Do not wait to be asked for the differential; it is part of the assessment.

In a clinical note

The same structure appears as the A in a SOAP note or the Assessment & Plan in an H&P. Write the problem representation as the opening sentence of the assessment, followed by your numbered differential with supporting reasoning. Each item in the plan should trace back to a specific problem in the assessment.

Deep Dive

The Plan

The plan is the action layer of your assessment. It is organized by problem — not by order type or timing — and every item in it should be traceable back to a specific diagnosis or concern in the assessment. A plan that cannot be linked to the assessment is a plan without a rationale.

Structure

Number each problem and group your orders, referrals, and follow-up under it. For each problem, think in terms of: what you are trying to confirm or rule out, what treatment or management you are initiating, what you are monitoring, and what the disposition or next step is.

Example — Plan
1. Rule out perforation: CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast. NPO pending imaging. IV access ×2. Surgical consult on standby.

2. Infectious workup: Blood cultures ×2, CBC with differential, CMP, lactate, CRP, urinalysis.

3. Empiric antibiotics: Piperacillin-tazobactam after cultures drawn. Acetaminophen for pain and fever. Hold NSAIDs.

4. Hold immunosuppression: Hold adalimumab and methotrexate; notify rheumatology.

5. Disposition: Admit for IV antibiotics and monitoring. Upgrade to surgical team if CT shows perforation or abscess.

In an oral presentation

Deliver the plan after the assessment, organized by problem. You do not need to read every order — summarize your thinking for each problem and name the key actions. The attending will ask for specifics if needed.

In a clinical note

The P in a SOAP note and the Plan in an H&P follow the same problem-based structure. Be specific: name orders with doses and timing where relevant, include follow-up plans, and make the link to the assessment explicit for each item. Avoid vague entries like "continue current management" without specifying what that means.

Section 5

Clinical Note Writing

For a subset of cases, you will write a note documenting the encounter. Unlike a presentation, a note is a durable record — complete, accurate, and structured for care coordination and medicolegal purposes. In clerkships you will use two main formats, each suited to a different moment in care.

H&P — History & Physical
When: new admissions · new patient visits CC & HPI
Chief complaint and full narrative of the presenting illness.

Past History
Medical history, surgical history, medications, allergies, family history, social history, and ROS — each as its own labeled section.

Physical Exam
Full exam documented by system, with vitals first.

Assessment & Plan
Diagnosis with reasoning and a problem-based plan.
SOAP Note
When: follow-up visits · interval progress notes S — Subjective
Chief concern, HPI, and relevant history (PMH, meds, allergies, family/social hx) folded in where pertinent. What the patient reports.

O — Objective
Vitals (always first), physical exam findings, labs, imaging, and other data. What you observe and measure.

A — Assessment
Your clinical interpretation — leading diagnosis with supporting findings, differential if uncertain.

P — Plan
Organized by problem. Orders, referrals, treatments, follow-up — each traceable to the assessment.

The key difference is structure, not content. A SOAP note does not omit family or social history — it folds them into the Subjective where relevant. An H&P surfaces them as labeled sections because the encounter is comprehensive by design. Most notes you write in this series will follow the SOAP format.

Tips for a good note
  • Place vital signs in the Objective section (not in the HPI)
  • Include pertinent negatives as clinical findings (e.g., "no rebound tenderness")
  • Prioritize and support diagnoses in the assessment (show your reasoning)
  • Link each plan item directly to the assessment with a clear rationale
  • Be specific in the plan (orders, doses, timing, follow-up)
  • Use clear, concise language (avoid repetition and unnecessary detail)

Writing the assessment

The A in a SOAP note and the Assessment & Plan in an H&P follow the same structure as the assessment in an oral presentation: open with a problem representation, state your leading diagnosis with supporting evidence, then work through your differential with explicit reasoning for each alternative. The plan is organized by problem and traces directly back to the assessment.

For the full framework — including how to build a problem representation, structure the differential, and connect the plan — see the Assessment & Problem Representation section. The examples there are drawn from a SOAP context and apply directly to note writing.

O — Objective

Vitals: T 38.4°C · HR 102 · BP 124/78 · RR 16 · SpO₂ 98% on room air
General: Alert, oriented, mild distress secondary to pain. Not toxic-appearing.
Abdomen: Soft. LLQ tenderness to palpation with voluntary guarding. No rebound tenderness. No palpable mass. Bowel sounds present in all quadrants. No distension.

A — Assessment

72-year-old immunocompromised man (adalimumab) presenting with acute LLQ pain with fever and tachycardia, consistent with an acute colonic process.

1. Acute diverticulitis — leading diagnosis. LLQ localization, fever, tachycardia are classic. Immunosuppression may blunt exam and laboratory response.
2. Colonic malignancy with perforation or obstruction — must rule out given age, family history of colon cancer at 65, and immunosuppression.
3. Infectious colitis — on differential given biologic use, though absence of diarrhea is less consistent.
4. Ischemic colitis — possible given age and tachycardia, but LLQ localization and absence of hematochezia make this less likely.

P — Plan

1. Rule out perforation: CT abdomen/pelvis with IV contrast. NPO pending imaging. IV access ×2. Surgical consult on standby.

2. Infectious workup: Blood cultures ×2, CBC with differential, CMP, LFTs, lactate, CRP, urinalysis.

3. Medications: Hold adalimumab and methotrexate; notify rheumatology. Empiric piperacillin-tazobactam after cultures drawn. Acetaminophen 650 mg IV for pain and fever. Hold NSAIDs.

4. Disposition: Admit for monitoring and IV antibiotics. Upgrade to surgical team if CT shows perforation or abscess.

A — Assessment

45-year-old woman with HFpEF, hospital day 3 for volume overload now improving, with new-onset tachyarrhythmia most consistent with atrial fibrillation with rapid ventricular response.

New AF likely triggered by physiologic stress and electrolyte abnormality (low Mg) in the setting of recent diuresis and underlying atrial enlargement from HFpEF.
2. Pulmonary embolism — On differential given hospitalization and new tachycardia. Against: no chest pain, hypoxia, or leg swelling; SpO₂ preserved; rhythm irregularly irregular rather than sinus tachycardia.
3. Acute coronary syndrome — On differential given tachycardia. Against: no chest pain, no ischemic EKG changes.
4. Infectious cause — Consider if no other etiology identified. Against: afebrile, no localizing symptoms.

1. Atrial fibrillation with RVR: Continue telemetry. Rate control — continue metoprolol, titrate to HR and BP. Replete magnesium IV (goal >2.0). Monitor potassium. Evaluate anticoagulation indication (CHA₂DS₂-VASc).

2. HFpEF, improving: Continue diuresis as tolerated. Daily weights and I/O trending.

3. Electrolyte abnormalities: Replete Mg IV. Recheck BMP in AM.

4. Disposition: Continue inpatient monitoring pending rate control and clinical stability.

Pilot Feature

Clinical Management

Iterative clinical management — the ongoing cycle of collecting information, interpreting it, and making decisions as the patient evolves — is at the core of what we do as clinicians. It is also one of the hardest things to practice as a student, because many learning cases end at the initial assessment and it is difficult to feel connected to the process of information flow when you cannot independently enter orders.

What management involves

Intentional orderingOrder tests and treatments with a clear clinical question in mind—what are you trying to confirm, rule out, or monitor?
Interpretation and reassessmentUse new data (labs, vitals, clinical response) to continually update and refine your assessment.
Triage and follow-upDetermine the appropriate level of care (admit vs. discharge) and set a clear follow-up plan and timeline.

How it works

After the interview and presentation, you enter a management interface where you order tests, review results, and adjust your plan as new data — labs, vitals, clinical updates — is released over time. In select cases, you will take on the role of the treating clinician over a simulated 24-hour period.

This feature is in pilot The management component is available in select cases. Feedback from this cohort will be used to refine the interface and expand it to additional cases. If you encounter unexpected behavior, use the feedback button within the case to flag it.
